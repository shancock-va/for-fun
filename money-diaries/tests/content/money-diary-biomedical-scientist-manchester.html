Money Diary: A Biomedical Scientist In Manchester On 42k

Anonymous
Last Updated 29 May 2020, 0:00
Welcome to Money Diaries, where we're tackling what might be the last taboo facing modern working women: money. We're asking a cross-section of women how they spend their hard-earned money during a seven-day period – and we're tracking every last penny.
This week: "I am an NHS hospital laboratory worker living in south Manchester with my partner and two fur babies. We bought our house three years ago and have been gradually doing it up ever since. I am a keen marathon runner so spent a large proportion of my free time this winter training in the freezing cold and rain. Now that races are cancelled for the foreseeable future, I wish I’d spent more of that time in pubs and restaurants with friends and family. Hindsight is a wonderful thing!
Advertisement
I’ve worked as a biomedical scientist for the last 10 years and have been in my current role for five. Working in a large specialist laboratory, I get to see a wide variety of cases and interact with a large cohort of staff. I think biomedical scientists and other laboratory workers are easily forgotten about as we are hidden away behind the scenes, but the testing we do helps inform around 80% of decisions made by the clinical area. The work of the laboratory is being highlighted more during the current pandemic so it is nice to see more recognition for our role (although I wish it wasn’t due to these circumstances)."
Occupation: Senior biomedical scientist
Industry: NHS
Age: 33
Location: Manchester
Salary: £41,723
Paycheque amount: Usually around £2,400 after tax, National Insurance, NHS pension (9.3%) and student loan contribution. The exact amount varies depending on how many out-of-hours or extra shifts I have worked the previous month.
Number of housemates: Three: two cats and one fiancé.
Monthly Expenses
Housing costs: My fiancé M and I live in a three-bed semi in south Manchester. My half of the mortgage is £378.
Loan payments: £2,400 on an interest-free credit card. The balance has gone up and down over the years, starting with funding my master’s degree 10 years ago. Then unexpected large bills arrive so I’ve never quite cleared it (my old and tired car being the main culprit, I just can’t get rid of him yet). I have two cards and always transfer the balance if I’m not able to pay it off in full prior to the interest-free period ending. I pay £200 each month to my credit card and £200 for student loan repayments, which should finally be gone by the end of the year.
Utilities: My half of all the bills works out at £72 for council tax, £16 for water, £40 for gas and electric, £25 for TV/phone/internet, £4 for Netflix and £15 for pet insurance. Along with our mortgage, these all come out of our joint account, which we both pay £1,000 into each month. The rest goes on food, bits for the house and garden and any fun things we want to do, which usually revolve around eating out with friends – something I am really missing at the moment! Any remaining money gets put into our savings or towards our mortgage.
Savings? I have approximately £10,000 in a joint savings account with my fiancé which is primarily our wedding fund. A lot of this was meant to be going out to our suppliers over the next month but sadly we have to postpone to next summer due to the pandemic. We will need to look into what is best to do with this money in the meantime and are continuing to add to the pot for the next year so we can start saving for further house improvements.
All other monthly expenses: £250 into the wedding fund/savings. £28 car insurance, £14 union membership, £14 Institute of Biomedical Science membership, £7.20 life insurance (why do my cats cost more than me?!), £6 bike insurance, £40 phone bill (I know I could find a better deal but I’m inherently lazy with this kind of thing, plus I now have unlimited data until October for being NHS so will probably sort it out after that ends) and £25.99 gym membership (currently on hold while the gym is closed). I drive to work and usually have to pay for parking (£32) but this has been suspended for the next three months while we all deal with the pandemic. Petrol/diesel we fund from the joint account but while M is also classed as a key worker, his job can be done at home so we are only spending about £30 a month at present.

Day One

7.15am: Alarm goes off and I am up, much to the annoyance of M. I cannot hit snooze, although my argument is why would you set your alarm any earlier than you physically have to be up? On a later shift today but want to make the most of my daily exercise so after tea and toast and a few house jobs I get changed into my running clothes and head out.

It’s a lovely morning for a run and very quiet on the roads. I am trying to be conscientious and avoid my normal routes down by the river as these have very narrow trails and an increase in foot traffic at the moment. Thankfully due to years of running I have developed excellent navigational skills so am confident my new route will bring me back to my house in six miles... 

10.15am: One wrong turn and eight miles later, I am now behind schedule. A quick shower and change and I’m in the car. Thankfully still no traffic so my normal 40 minute commute is halved and I make it in with five minutes to spare!

11am: No major issues to contend with when I arrive and all urgent work is being attended to. I check in on my team then spend the morning checking reports, reviewing quality control data and working on some training programmes I am developing. A lot of projects I was due to oversee have been put on hold while we prioritise our COVID response so I’m taking the time to clear some tasks that have been on my job list for a while when the laboratory is well staffed and I’m not needed to perform bench work.

2pm: Forgot my lunch in the hurry this morning. To avoid unnecessary exposure to the outside world I’ve been trying to do one large supermarket shop a week only but today I will have to go to the onsite M&S. Feel guilty for my lack of organisation but manage to get a reduced sandwich, fruit and crisps (£4) and eat lunch in the hospital garden enjoying the sunshine. Remember to send some money to a friend I have arranged to buy flowers with – it was meant to be a mutual friend’s wedding day tomorrow (gorgeous weather forecast too, typical) so wanted to get her a little gift (£12). Feel sad for her as I know the emotional rollercoaster of wedding postponement all too well, but also know it’s the right thing to do and there’s a bigger picture right now. Though I wouldn’t wish this on others, I will admit it is nice to have someone who totally understands how I’m feeling to moan to on days when I’m feeling a bit bridezilla.

4.30pm: A quick drink break and I pop an email to our wedding venue to ask how to pay the final balance. Although we have postponed for a year with no additional charge they have asked we pay at the original scheduled dates. I’ve seen some horror stories from other brides so feel lucky that we will not be out of pocket as all our suppliers have moved date for free, too. While replying to confirm our payment schedule, my sister C messages to ask what I want for my birthday. She is far more fashion conscious than I will ever be (just give me jeans and a T-shirt any day) so I ask for some item of clothing now that summer appears to be heading our way.

8pm: Hand over to the night staff then head home to make us a late tea as M was working late too. I prepped some pizza dough last night using the recipe from our favourite local pizzeria. While not as good as their offering, I’m pretty pleased with the end result! M shows his approval by finishing his then polishing off the rest of mine. 

10.30pm: Finish the day by watching a film (I’m committing to the lockdown MCU (Marvel Cinematic Universe) marathon as this requires minimal thinking and contains maximum Chris Hemsworth). Feeling full and sleepy, we decide to head to bed.

Total: £16
2 of 8
Day Two

7.30am: It’s the weekend! Sadly I get a grand total of 15 minutes lie-in as the cats like their food on a tight schedule and will come and sit on your chest and shout to get you up if they deem it unacceptably late.

8am: Admit defeat and feed cats. M makes us scrambled egg on toast for breakfast which I eat while messaging my friend about her wedding. They have a lovely day planned so I’m glad she is in good spirits.

9.45am: Head out for a run later than I normally would on a Saturday. Eight very hot miles, I knew I should have gone earlier! I shouldn’t complain though as this is Manchester so it's unlikely to last long. 

2pm: Spending the day helping M pottering around the garden – we are using lockdown as an excuse to tackle it (or rather we have run out of excuses not to tackle it). Use the remaining dough to make us a pizza, served with a side salad to break up the beige and promote it to a grown-up lunch.

4pm: Catch sight of my dishevelled self in the mirror and immediately order some hair dye, shampoo and conditioner online (£22.99). I’ve been holding off but needs must and I can’t wait until July to cover these greys, not while I’m still going out to work every day. A lot of the guys I work with have got their partners to cut their hair; I have told M there isn’t a chance I’m going near his!

9pm: Head to our friends' virtual 'local' for the Saturday night pub quiz, Aperol Spritz in hand (it has felt like summer today so I’m fully embracing it). M has really good general knowledge whereas I am good for health, science and '90s pop trivia only but it seems to be working well for us so far. I’m really enjoying this weekly Zoom/Kahoot meeting, as so many of our friends now have young families and we are all so busy that we rarely get to meet up all together anymore.

12am: Have drunk far too many Aperol Spritzes but have also won the quiz! Agree to be next weekend’s quizmasters then say goodnight, leave the 'pub' and head to bed.

Total: £22.99

Day Three

8am: Goodbye sunshine. The weather has turned already and it is grey, windy and cold outside, which does nothing to help motivate me to shake off my Aperol-induced headache. Decide against going out for a run and stay in bed snoozing and chatting with M (and the cats). The Sunday morning long run is ingrained in any runner's training diary and it is such a routine for me that I still feel guilty when I don’t go, really need to shake this off! Especially as it is unlikely we will be racing again this year. Vow to work on running when I want to rather than feel I have to, as it is ultimately a wonderful stress-relieving activity which I feel very fortunate to still be allowed to do. 

2pm: Spend the day baking a cake (carrot), cleaning the kitchen (because icing gets everywhere, no matter how hard you try), catching up with family WhatsApp groups and deciding on our quiz categories for next weekend (top tip: 'bad fan art' can keep you amused for hours).

5pm: After adding the final touches to our quiz we catch the afternoon government announcement: there will be a statement from the PM on Monday regarding lifting lockdown restrictions. This announcement gives me some hope that we might actually get to go on our UK-based ‘minimoon’ at the end of the summer (the one thing from the wedding we haven’t yet cancelled or postponed because we need something to look forward to).

7.30pm: M has made a big batch of turkey chilli to serve with jacket potatoes, which we follow with (another) slice of the carrot cake. Spend the rest of the evening half watching TV and half crocheting the next baby blanket on my list. I’ve been trying to make one for all my friends’ new little ones but can’t always keep up, depending on how many are due! A small silver lining of lockdown is that it's making me more likely to stay on track with these now I’m spending more time in the house.

10.30pm: Running out of wool and can’t be bothered to go upstairs to find another ball of that shade so bed it is.

Total: £0
4 of 8
Day Four

6.30am: Back to work and I’m up earlier to do the weekly supermarket big shop. Prior to lockdown we would visit a couple of supermarkets to do smaller shops every three or four days but we’ve had to change our habits and plan food more carefully. Feel very grateful that Sainsbury’s has a half-hour NHS and carers slot which means I can get everything done quickly without queuing and without exposure to many other people. Like a lot of NHS workers I’m aware of my own higher risk of getting the virus but I don’t want to then pass that on to those who are more vulnerable (I know some supermarkets have an NHS and pensioners hour, which is a nice idea terribly executed – two groups of people you want to be keeping separate if possible). £19.70 for my half, which should see us through into next week.

9am: In work, review our workload and set the team their tasks. We had some samples arrive overnight for a patient requiring urgent surgery who has a potential bleeding disorder so this is our priority for the day.  Asked to cover an early shift tomorrow due to sickness and a colleague pulls in a favour and I agree to cover his weekend shift, which has fallen on his birthday. Working in a 24/7 environment there’s a lot of shift juggling to manage the rota but we all help each other out where we can.

5pm: Head home on time. M is coordinating the late shift so still working when I get home. I decide to go out on the long run I planned on doing yesterday, a nice 12 miles listening to podcasts and forgetting about the world. When I’m back and showered, Boris is making his announcement laying out the plans to lift lockdown restrictions so M is feeling the pressure: he has a lot of news to cover and get onto the page for international viewing by millions. Feeling very proud of my future husband I leave him to it and heat up our leftovers from last night for tea, which he will hopefully be able to break off and join me for. Have a spurt of energy so decide to make some pasta salads for lunch for the rest of the week too. I always feel so smug and adult when I prep lunches under normal circumstances – at the moment I feel it is something I have to do to reduce shop trips so I’m hoping it will become a habit!

10.30pm: Head to bed to mindlessly scroll through Instagram for a while before eventually falling asleep

Total: £19.70
5 of 8
Day Five

5.50am: You know your alarm is going off early when the cats refuse to get up with you. Not sure why but I feel a pre-6am alarm so much more than one set 10/15 minutes later. 

7am: Working at a different hospital site this morning as cover. Once I’ve set up the lab, I read the latest government document on COVID-19 while waiting for my reagents.

9.30am: I hand over to the day team and head to the main site for the rest of my shift, getting a card for a colleague on the way who has recently suffered a bereavement (£7 including collection money for flowers). 

3pm: Work has been busier today (although not quite back up to pre-COVID levels as a lot of our work comes from outpatient and GP appointments). We help diagnose a newborn baby with a bleeding disorder and I pass the results through to the paediatric haematology consultant before I finish for the day. 

3.30pm: The support of the public and local businesses for the NHS during this crisis has been wonderful. Our trust has been gifted the use of a sports centre at a local football club, which has been transformed into a relaxation and wellbeing site. I head down to use the facilities and have a lovely (socially distant) chat with the manager. It’s true that testing times bring out the best and worst in people and I’m lucky to say I’ve definitely had more dealings with the former during this pandemic. 

6pm: M finished work early so has cleaned the house and made a bean, chorizo and spinach stew for tea. I’m really appreciating him working from home at the minute! Although we have discussed how we might be finding things quite different if I were working from home too...

Total: £7
6 of 8
Day Six

7.30am: Back on a late shift today (my shifts aren’t usually this varied in a week but we are losing staff due to isolation/shielding precautions and it’s not like I’m doing much else at the moment) so get up, feed the cats and head out for a run, thankfully not getting lost this time and therefore having adequate time to get ready for work at 11. 

10.45am: We have run out of milk so I pop to M&S to buy some before my shift starts, justifying this as an essential purchase. Not sure the iced spiced buns I bought to share with my work wife (WW) count as essential but it was all one trip (£2.30).

12.30pm: Share buns with WW at lunch. We discuss all things work and non-work, including how the dynamics of staff relationships have definitely changed during this period. We are becoming much more of a support system for each other now the outside world is more cut off. I’m very lucky to have WW for support as well as M, it makes being away from other family and friends so much easier to bear. Head back into work to an email that more goodies have been delivered to us from external businesses via the trust charity. Stock up on biscuits, chocolate, hand cream and a new water bottle.

4.30pm: Have been thinking about all the NHS gifting today. I feel a bit conflicted as it is an amazing gesture for all of us while we remain in work, however we all have secure employment and aren’t struggling to make ends meet like a lot of people will be right now. Decide to donate to a local homeless charity online (£10).

7.30pm: Leave work with a birthday present from WW and a homemade brownie from another work friend, life is good!

Total: £12.30
7 of 8
Day Seven

7.30am: Today is my birthday! Wake up to an excitable M (plus cats) greeting me with cards, flowers and the news that my favourite local bakery has reopened and he has ordered me a birthday cake. 

9am: Feeling very emotional (in a good way) as people have put a lot of thought and effort into my gifts and cards. Get a lovely perfume from my mum and dad so decide to order myself a couple of matching candles (£30, M pays for half as a birthday treat).

9.30am: FaceTime my friend S and her little girl for a birthday catch-up to thank her for my flowers and eat a brownie for breakfast while chatting (it is an unwritten rule that there can be no judgement on birthday meals, just like Christmas Day).

12.30pm: Birthday cake has arrived and it looks incredible. I can’t wait to eat some but as I want to head out for a run in the sunshine I decide that double chocolate orange sponge won’t sit well on my stomach so it can wait until I’m back.

2pm: Decide to exercise some restraint and save the birthday cake for my family Zoom chat and quiz this evening. 

2.30pm: Eat half a box of birthday chocolate as a compromise while continuing with my MCU marathon. Cat 1 joins me for a belly rub and a snooze; he is loving the extra attention he is getting at the moment due to us both being home so much more often.

7pm: M has finished work and we order takeaway from a local Asian restaurant. We rarely get takeaway but do tend to eat out at least once every couple of weeks. We order chicken gyoza, nasi goreng and yaki udon which we devour quickly before our family Zoom (£32 from joint account).

8pm: Finally have some birthday cake while taking part in a birthday-themed quiz compiled by C and her boyfriend. As expected my parents were useless on the '90s round! Finish off with a round of "Happy birthday" and say our goodbyes. Really missing everyone but as birthdays in lockdown go, I’d say this one has been pretty good.

10.30pm: Head to bed.

Total: £30
8 of 8
The Breakdown

Food/Drink: £26
Clothes/Beauty: £22.99
Entertainment: £0
Travel: £0
Other: £59

Total: £107.99

Conclusion

"This was a fairly typical week of spending for me, although I suspect that out of lockdown at least one meal out would have appeared on the list, plus I would be spending more on travel. I tend to save pretty well day to day then splash out on a bigger item I’ve had my eye on. Reading the diary back I’m incredibly grateful for the interactions I’m still able to have with friends and family, and that I’m able to still run outside. I have also realised I need to stop feeling so guilty all the time, we are all doing our best in the current situation and I should accept all gifts graciously as people obviously want to get behind the NHS."
